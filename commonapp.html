The Common Application


The Common Application is how most high school students apply to colleges and universities; there are other ways to apply (such as the Coalition for Access and Affordability application), but the Common App is the most popular, and the main reason why is because you can send out all the information colleges need from one central site and because most colleges accept it. (If you want to visit the website, go to commonapp.org.) Every August 1st, a new version of the application is available and a section of old data gets deleted; answers in the “Common App” tab (Profile, Family, Education, Testing, Activities, and Writing) roll over to the new year, but information for college-specific supplements, release authorizations, FERPA selections, recommender invitations, assignments, and forms, PDFs of applications and writing supplements, responses to removed questions, and responses to questions that may have a different format or different response options will be deleted.

Students can create an account on the Common Application whenever; there isn’t a requirement that accounts must only belong to seniors, so even freshmen can make accounts and start filling out their basic information in the “Common App” tab— they just need to make sure to roll over their accounts every year. You can revise the application as many times as you’d like, and you can even edit your application and send out different versions of it to different colleges. If you don’t want to create an account just yet but would like to take a look around the site, you can create a “practice applicant” account. Note that creating an account is free, but most colleges often have an application fee; if you can’t pay the fee, talk to your counselor—there are numerous scholarships that help lessen the cost.

In the Common App, there are five essay topics that college applicants can pick from, and most colleges require this essay; one tip of ours is to look at them early on so that you can edit and refine the essay without feeling rushed or failing to see crucial mistakes— if you want to be as competitive as you can be, it’s best to have someone reliable give their feedback on each draft of the essay. However, please be sure that the words are yours! Colleges want to get a glimpse of who you are as a person, and they can tell when you aren’t being sincere or if the words are someone else’s.

In addition to the Common App essay, most colleges will ask you to answer additional questions, write college-specific essays, and submit recommendation letters; all of them will ask for information about your high school— give those forms to your counselors and teachers for them to fill out! They only have to fill out the forms once, but it’s very important that the colleges receive them.

Tips:

Do everything as early as possible so that you will be able to fix any mistakes that may pop up. Sometimes, a missed fee, question, or section of information may suddenly grab your attention-- it's best to be prepared as possible for college applications.

Start with the simple items first (like your basic information), but take a look at the essay prompts and spend some time thinking about them before you start writing; gather your thoughts and then write them all down-- that is Draft 0, it’s not even Draft 1! Start editing and revising until you and a reliable proofreader are both satisfied with your essay.

The deadline for regular decision is between January 1st and February 1st for most colleges; early action and early decision deadlines are even earlier! Your teachers and counselors will most probably have a multitude of students asking for recommendation letters, December is a time of midterms and winter break, and some teachers even have some specific requirements you’d have to fulfill before they write your recommendation letter (for example, some teachers want to personally meet up with the student). If your teacher is extremely popular or you know that they have a cap on the number of students they'll write recommendation letters for, ask them in the spring of your junior year; otherwise, ask them as soon as school starts! A more laid-back (but slightly dangerous) option would be to ask them about four weeks before the application is due.

Create an ordered checklist on what you plan to do; make sure that you don't miss anything, and review the websites on the colleges (and potentially special programs, like a nursing or engineering program) so that you know you fulfill all of their requirements.

When you are writing your essay, keep the word count in mind; it's helpful to plan out your essay beforehand, so that you know you are getting all of the important information in.
